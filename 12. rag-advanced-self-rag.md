# Self-RAG: Learning to Retrieve, Generate, and Critique through Self-Reflection

*Asai et al., University of Washington & IBM Research AI — ICLR 2024 (Oral, Top 1%)*

---

## The Core Problem

Standard LLMs are like students who can only answer from memory — they’re limited to whatever knowledge was baked into their training weights. Traditional RAG tries to fix this by always handing the student a stack of books (retrieved passages) before answering, but this can backfire. If the question is simple, like “What year was the iPhone released?”, the extra books just add noise. Worse, even when the right book is provided — say a passage that clearly states “The patient does not have diabetes” — the model might still ignore it and hallucinate “Yes, the patient has diabetes.” Self-RAG solves both problems by teaching the model to be selective and reflective. For example, if asked “What is 2+2?”, it knows retrieval isn’t needed and answers directly. But if asked “Explain the OAuth authentication flow,” it decides to retrieve relevant technical passages, generates a draft, and then critiques its own answer against the retrieved evidence to ensure accuracy. This way, Self-RAG avoids irrelevant retrieval and enforces consistency with the sources, making outputs both cleaner and more trustworthy.

## The Simple Version, No Jargon :: The problem with a regular AI (no RAG)

Imagine a student taking a test with **no textbook allowed** — only what they memorized. If they memorized wrong, or forgot something, they'll confidently write a wrong answer. That's a normal LLM: it only knows what it learned during training, and it can "misremember" (hallucinate).

### The problem with regular RAG (always open-book)

Now imagine the student is **forced to open a textbook for every single question**, even "What is 2+2?" That's a waste of time. And worse — sometimes the textbook page clearly says one thing, but the nervous student still writes the wrong answer anyway, ignoring the book completely. So handing over the book doesn't guarantee a correct answer.

### What Self-RAG does differently

Self-RAG is like a **smart student who has learned three extra skills**:

1. **"Do I even need the book?"**
   For easy questions ("2+2?"), it just answers from memory. For harder or fact-specific questions ("What's this patient's diagnosis?"), it decides to look something up.

2. **"Is this the right page?"**
   After grabbing a passage, it checks whether that passage is actually relevant, instead of blindly using whatever was handed to it.

3. **"Does my answer actually match what the book says?"**
   Before finalizing the answer, it double-checks its own draft against the retrieved text — did I actually use what the passage said, or did I contradict it? If the passage says "no diabetes" but the draft says "has diabetes," this checking step is meant to catch that mismatch.

### The key trick behind how it does this

It's not just "thinking harder." The model is specially **trained to output little tags** while writing its answer, like:
- 🏷️ "should I retrieve? yes/no"
- 🏷️ "is this passage relevant? yes/no"
- 🏷️ "is my answer supported by the passage? fully / partially / not at all"

These tags let it grade its own multiple draft answers and pick the best-supported one — like a student writing a few possible answers, checking each against the textbook, and submitting the one that's best backed up by evidence.

### One-line summary

> Self-RAG teaches the model **when to look things up, whether what it found is useful, and whether its own answer actually agrees with what it found** — instead of blindly retrieving every time and hoping the model pays attention.


## The Central Idea: Reflection Tokens

Self-RAG teaches a language model to think about its own thinking while answering. Instead of retrieval being something bolted on outside the model, retrieval becomes part of the model’s learned behavior. The trick is the use of reflection tokens — special signals the model generates during its reasoning. These tokens let the model pause and ask itself questions like: “Do I need to look something up?” or “Is this passage relevant?” or “Does my answer match the evidence?” or even “How good is my response overall?”

For example, if the model is asked “What year was the iPhone released?”, it might generate a Retrieve token, decide it doesn’t need external info, and answer directly: “2007.” If asked “Explain the OAuth authentication flow,” it could generate a Retrieve token, fetch technical passages, then use ISREL to check if those passages are relevant. After drafting an answer, it might generate ISSUP to verify that its explanation is supported by the retrieved text. Finally, it could output ISUSE=5 to signal that the response is complete and useful.

This is the conceptual leap: retrieval, evaluation, and critique all happen inside one unified generation pass. The model isn’t just spitting out text — it’s actively deciding when to consult external knowledge, checking its own consistency, and rating its own output. That makes Self-RAG controllable, trustworthy, and adaptable to different tasks.

There are four types of reflection tokens, each answering a distinct question:

| Token | Question Asked |
|---|---|
| `Retrieve` | Should I fetch external passages at all? |
| `ISREL` | Is the retrieved passage relevant to the query? |
| `ISSUP` | Is my generated output actually *supported* by the passage? |
| `ISUSE` | How useful is this response overall (1–5)? |

Generating reflection tokens makes the LM controllable during the inference phase, enabling it to tailor its behavior to diverse task requirements.

## The Inference Algorithm

At each generation step, the model follows a principled decision loop:

1. **Retrieval Decision** — The model predicts a `Retrieve` token (`yes / no / continue`). If no retrieval is needed (e.g., a creative writing prompt), it simply generates the next segment.
2. **Parallel Evidence Evaluation** — If retrieval is triggered, multiple passages are retrieved and processed *in parallel*. For each passage, the model predicts `ISREL` — filtering out irrelevant ones before they can corrupt the generation.
3. **Grounded Generation** — The model generates candidate response segments, one per passage.
4. **Self-Critique and Ranking** — `ISSUP` checks whether the segment is fully, partially, or not supported by its passage. `ISUSE` scores overall response quality. Candidates are ranked by a weighted combination of these scores, and the best segment is selected.

This process differs from conventional RAG, which consistently retrieves a fixed number of documents regardless of retrieval necessity and never revisits generation quality. Moreover, Self-RAG provides citations for each segment with its self-assessment of whether the output is supported by the passage, leading to easier fact verification.

## How It's Trained: A Two-Model System

Self-RAG requires training two models: a **Critic** and a **Generator**.

**The Critic Model (C):** Manual annotation of reflection tokens for each segment is expensive. A state-of-the-art LLM like GPT-4 can be effectively used to generate such feedback. The authors collect 4k–20k supervised training data for each reflection token type by prompting GPT-4, then distill this knowledge into an in-house critic model. The critic achieves >90% agreement with GPT-4 predictions on most token categories — meaning the distilled model captures GPT-4's judgment without requiring it at inference time.

**The Generator Model (M):** The generator is trained on a curated corpus with interleaving passages retrieved by a retriever R and reflection tokens predicted by the critic model C. Reflection tokens are inserted into the original corpus *offline* by the trained critic. This eliminates the need to host a critic model during training, reducing overhead. The generator then learns to emit these tokens naturally as part of next-token prediction — the reflection tokens simply become part of the model's expanded vocabulary.

## Why This Matters Conceptually

The key insight Self-RAG encodes is that **retrieval is not always helpful**, and **generation quality is not self-evident**. Most RAG pipelines treat these as assumptions; Self-RAG treats them as learned variables. This has several architectural implications:

- **Adaptive retrieval** means the model can handle queries ranging from those requiring zero external knowledge to those needing multiple retrieval steps within a single response.
- **Self-critique** decouples generation from evaluation — the model develops an internal auditor that runs alongside its generative process.
- **Controllable inference** — because behavior is governed by reflection token probabilities, users can tune retrieval frequency, trade off citation precision vs. completeness, or enforce hard constraints at test time without retraining.

The inference-time algorithm enables flexibly adjusting retrieval frequency for different downstream applications and customizing model behaviors to user preferences by leveraging reflection tokens through segment-level beam search using a weighted linear sum of the reflection token probabilities as the segment score.

---

Self-RAG is less a retrieval system and more a framework for making a model **epistemically self-aware** — teaching it to know what it knows, know when it doesn't, and verify whether what it says is actually grounded. That's the conceptual core.
